# Design Spec: "What Porn Does to Your Brain?"

**Date:** 2026-05-09
**Channel:** SENSUM — Science of Kindness
**Status:** Approved for implementation

---

## Context

This is the second SENSUM video. The first video ("The Science of Feeling Behind in Life") established the channel's three-mechanism format, peer-review standard, and brand voice. This video applies the same structure to a topic that carries significant moral baggage and popular misinformation — pornography's psychological effects.

The goal is not to moralize or validate, but to do what the channel does best: replace shame-fog with science, and offer a compassionate, evidence-based framework. The topic is chosen because the gap between popular narrative ("porn addiction is an epidemic") and peer-reviewed reality (contested, nuanced, largely about moral incongruence) is exactly the kind of gap SENSUM exists to close.

### Hook (~90–120 sec)

Open on the experience — not the behavior. The viewer who googled this topic and felt a flash of shame before clicking. Name it without judgment, hold it, then make the pivot:

> "Before we talk about brains, let's talk about what brought you here."

Immediately address the scientific controversy: "Porn addiction" does not appear in the DSM-5. Researchers disagree. This video will show you what the science *does* agree on — and why that matters more than the debate.

**Tone target:** The viewer feels safe to keep watching. No moralism, no validation theater.

---

### Mechanism 1 — Reward Sensitization (~4 min)

**The science:** Dopamine reward circuit research, superstimulus hypothesis, neuroplasticity claims.

**Honest framing:** The popular "your brain on porn" narrative (NoFap literature, Gary Wilson's work) overclaims the neuroimaging evidence. Peer-reviewed research (Prause et al., 2015; Steele et al., 2013) shows that self-reported problems don't reliably map to neural reward hyposensitivity. What *is* supported: habitual use can shift attentional bias and arousal thresholds — but this is behavior-dependent, not universal.

**Key claim (verifiable):** High-frequency users show altered attentional processing toward sexual cues (Mechelmans et al., 2014), but this is not equivalent to addiction-level neural change in most users.

**PubMed search targets:**
- Prause N, Steele VR, Staley C, Sabatinelli D (2015) — EEG/LPP study
- Mechelmans DJ et al. (2014) — attentional bias study
- Banca P et al. (2016) — novelty bias neuroimaging

---

### Mechanism 2 — Moral Incongruence (~4 min)

**The science:** This is the strongest and most replicable finding in the field. Joshua Grubbs and colleagues have demonstrated across multiple studies that perceived addiction to pornography — the distress, the sense of being out of control — is predicted more strongly by religious/moral belief about pornography than by actual frequency of use.

**Core insight:** The shame you feel may have more to do with who you believe you *should* be than what you're actually doing. This is not permission to do anything; it's a precision tool for understanding where the pain is actually coming from.

**Key claims (verifiable):**
- Grubbs JB et al. (2015) — "Porn addiction" as a function of moral incongruence
- Grubbs JB et al. (2019) — replication and extension
- Willoughby BJ et al. (2016) — relationship between religiosity and perceived pornography addiction

**PubMed search targets:** Grubbs moral incongruence pornography; perceived addiction pornography religiosity

---

### Mechanism 3 — Intimacy Displacement (~3 min)

**The science:** Relationship research on pornography use and partner outcomes. Two effects documented in the literature: (1) solitary use in relationship contexts correlates with lower sexual satisfaction and intimacy for both partners in some studies; (2) the effect is moderated by partner awareness, mutual use, and relationship quality.

**Honest framing:** Correlation, not causation. The mechanism is likely not the pornography itself but the displacement of relational investment and the secrecy that often accompanies it.

**Key claims (verifiable):**
- Perry SL & Schleifer C (2018) — longitudinal data on porn use and relationship quality
- Willoughby BJ, Carroll JS, Busby DM (2012) — partner satisfaction
- Maddox AM et al. (2011) — relationship functioning and sexual satisfaction

**PubMed search targets:** pornography use relationship satisfaction longitudinal; solitary pornography use intimacy

---

### Prescription (~2 min)

Three actionable steps grounded in research findings above:

1. **Audit the gap, not the number** — Is the distress coming from frequency, or from a mismatch between behavior and values? Name which one it is. (Grubbs moral incongruence framework)
2. **Examine what the habit substitutes** — Loneliness, avoidance, stress regulation. The behavior isn't the root. (Attachment/substitution literature)
3. **If use feels compulsive:** Behavioral approaches with evidence base — CBT for hypersexual behavior, working with a therapist familiar with Grubbs' framework rather than abstinence-only models

---

### Outro (~30–45 sec)

Close on the humanity, not the behavior.

> "You watched this for a reason. That reason matters more than how many times you've watched anything else."

Reinforce: shame is a signal, not a sentence. The science gives you a map. What you do with it is yours.

## Research Standards

- All claims must be VERIFIED against peer-reviewed sources via `agent2_verify.py`
- "Porn addiction" framing must always be qualified (not DSM-recognized; contested in literature)
- Use language: "problematic use," "compulsive sexual behavior," "perceived addiction" — not "addiction" alone
- Gary Wilson / NoFap sources are NOT acceptable primary sources
- Psychology Today, WebMD, news articles: NOT acceptable
- Acceptable: PubMed-indexed journals (Archives of Sexual Behavior, JSAD, Journal of Sex Research)

## Visual Strategy

All images follow the SENSUM Scientific Etching style (see `workflows/style_guide_images.md`):
- **Colors:** #F4E5CA background + #582F0E ink only
- **Style:** 19th-century scientific engraving, cross-hatching, fine-liner
- **No text, no labels, no faces**
- **Character:** Faceless gender-neutral mannequin, emotion via posture only

**Scene direction for sensitive content:**
- All intimate/relational scenes conveyed through symbolic abstraction (two figures, distance/proximity, hands, objects)
- No explicit or suggestive imagery — the etching style naturally enforces this
- Brain/neuroscience scenes: anatomical diagrams, neural pathway sketches, reward circuit abstract maps
- Shame scenes: figure in contracted posture, heavy shadow cross-hatching, compressed space
- Resolution scenes: open posture, expanded negative space, single prop (book, door, hand extended)

**Estimated image count:** 60–75 images (consistent with first video's 76)

## Success Criteria

- Three mechanisms each have ≥2 peer-reviewed, PubMed-indexed sources with DOIs
- Script passes agent2 verification with 0 REMOVED claims and ≤2 FLAGGED claims
- Moral incongruence framing (Grubbs) is the emotional center of Mechanism 2
- Hook names the shame experience within the first 90 seconds
- No use of "addiction" without qualification
- Outro lands on the viewer's agency, not their behavior
